Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

This section explores key patterns in the dataset to uncover factors influencing student dropout rates. We analyze demographic, academic, and financial features to provide insights that will inform model development.

The target column reveals the dropout status of students. We analyze the distribution of students who dropped out, graduated, or are still enrolled.

Observation: 49% of students are graduates, while 32.1% have dropped out and 17% are still enrolled.

The age distribution is right-skewed, with most students in their late teens to early twenties. There is a noticeable increase in dropouts among students in their mid-20s to early 30s.

Observation: Dropout rates rise significantly for students in their mid-20s to early 30s.

The gender distribution shows that 64.8% of students are female, but the dropout rate is higher among males (45.1%) compared to females (25.1%).

  • Female Students (64.8%): Dropout: 701, Graduate: 548, Enrolled: 307
  • Male Students (35.2%): Dropout: 720, Graduate: 1661, Enrolled: 487

Observation: Males have a higher dropout rate despite representing a smaller portion of the student population.

We examine how financial factors like debt, tuition payment, and scholarship status correlate with dropout rates.

Observation: Students with outstanding debt (62%) or unpaid tuition (86.6%) have high dropout rates, while scholarship recipients have a low dropout rate (12.2%).

Our EDA reveals key insights into the factors influencing student dropouts, such as age, gender, and financial status. These findings will guide model development.

Data Preparation

Data preparation is a crucial step in ensuring model accuracy and efficiency. This phase focuses on feature selection, multicollinearity reduction, and dimensionality reduction for cleaner analysis and predictions.

The correlation heatmap below shows the relationships between features. High correlation values suggest potential multicollinearity.

To address this, we applied **Principal Component Analysis (PCA)** to the curricular units' data, retaining only the most significant components. We also dropped highly correlated features to streamline the dataset.

Observation:

  • Removed redundant features related to parents' occupation and qualifications.
  • Reduced curricular units data using PCA to retain only the most relevant variance.

Following correlation analysis, the following features were removed to reduce redundancy and multicollinearity:

  • Nationality
  • Mother's Occupation
  • Father's Qualification
  • Curricular Units (1st & 2nd Sem): Enrolled, Credited, Evaluations, Approved, Grade, Without Evaluations

These features were dropped to avoid model overfitting and ensure a more interpretable dataset.
